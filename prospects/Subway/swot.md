# SWOT — Subway (Senior AEM & React / Next.js Software Engineer)

## Strengths

- **Household brand** with incentive to invest in digital experiences that scale to many markets and franchisees.
- **Clear stack story** in the posting: AEM + Next.js with explicit emphasis on performance, SEO, and accessibility—easier to assess day-to-day work than a vague “full stack” requisition.
- **Remote US** opens talent access; comp range is stated for transparency.

## Weaknesses

- **Enterprise + franchise model** can mean many stakeholders, brand guardrails, and slower consensus than a small product company—pace may not match “startup” expectations.
- **Niche CMS** (AEM) creates a **skill moat**: strong candidates may be rare; ramps can be heavy if the team expects immediate deep AEM authoring, SPA Editor, and cloud pipeline expertise.

## Opportunities

- **Modernize** public web on Next.js while keeping **content governance** in AEM—good alignment if the org is serious about CWV and SSR/SSG.
- **Pattern reuse** from other large QSR / retail digital programs—possible internal momentum for **design systems** and **component libraries** once integration stabilizes.

## Threats

- Hiring for **AEM + Next together** is competitive; candidates from digital agencies or large Adobe shops may have **more direct AEM badges** than a strong React/Next generalist.
- Iconic brands attract volume applicants; standing out may depend on **concrete shipping examples** and **integration architecture** stories.

## Notes

- **Verify in interview:** Production AEM version (Cloud Service vs 6.x), **SPA Editor vs pure headless**, team ownership of **AEM components** vs a separate platform team, on-call/production expectations, and **realistic** Core Web Vitals targets.
- **Honest gap:** Profile emphasizes **headless CMS and GraphQL** with **DatoCMS/custom CMS**, not **Adobe AEM**—prepare a short story on how you’d **learn AEM-specific authoring, dispatcher, and deployment** without overstating prior tenure.
